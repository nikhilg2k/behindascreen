What The Pegasus Project revelations mean for privacy in the future

by Nikhil Gupta Jul 24,2021 · 5 min read

The Pegasus Spyware Scandal has rekindled the privacy debate. In recent years this debate has centered around Big Tech and user data privacy. Although violation of user privacy by corporations is an important issue that must be investigated and settled, corporate use of consumer data has mostly been limited to innocuous targeted advertisements. We now have a far more sinister matter that has not received the urgent and unadulterated attention that it demands. This is the violation of citizen privacy by the state and the threat it poses to the cherished ideals of our democratic republic.

No nation is oblivious to the destructive potential of cyberspace. This is evident from the fact that many countries classify cyberattacks as the greatest threat to national security. Any technology has the potential to be weaponized. This is exactly what the Israeli NSO has achieved in the form of Pegasus Spyware.

What is Pegasus Spyware? It is perhaps one of the most sophisticated, military grade cyber-weapon that we know of. Once the spyware infiltrates a device it gains access to almost all data on the device including messages, call logs, keystrokes, photos as well as the device microphone, camera and location. There has always been clamor amongst the public about facial recognition software being used in street cameras but a weapon like Pegasus can turn the devices that every person holds in their hands into a dedicated spy cam and location tracker making it the ultimate weapon of surveillance and espionage. What makes Pegasus even more dangerous is the fact that it has the ability to use non-interactive modes of infiltration. A link that you didn't click or a message that you didn't open can still install the spyware on your device without you ever realizing something is awry. Reports suggest that even a missed WhatsApp call that you may not pay any heed to may be enough to compromise your device.

The power that this technology holds: It has been argued that such a weapon can be a boon for national and public security because it can be utilized to surveil terrorist activities or to bust trafficking or pedophilia rings. When used with conscientious intentions it can thwart heinous terrorist activities at their roots. However, the revelations of The Pegasus Project expose how this weapon can and has been used by repressive governments to spy on critics and journalists. It is unfortunate and dismaying to find India on the list of NSO clients amongst countries that are infamous for undemocratic activities. As it is a developing story, the extent to which Pegasus has been used to target and surveil journalists so far is not yet clear but it is indubitable from the detailed forensic reports published by the media consortium that the spyware has indeed been misused for purposes that are far from fighting terrorism. Even in India the phones of 40 journalists have allegedly been targeted. It is undeniable that this technology must be utilized as best as possible to fight terrorism and abuse but being a potential threat to national security, it must be subject to maximum safeguards against misuse, similar to any other weapon of mass destruction.

Should we be concerned? It is of grave importance that every citizen be cognizant of the dire repercussions of the exploitation of such technology by the state. It is said that information is power and this weapon can fetch the details of the ins and outs of any person's daily life. It can be used as an impediment to free and fair journalism, as a tool for blackmailing constitutional offices including the judiciary and for dismantling the opposition to the ruling establishment, all the while remaining undetected. In the hands of an authoritarian government, this can serve as the last straw needed for the construction and management of a dystopian surveillance state like China’s atrocities in Xinjiang. Oftentimes it is argued that a lawful citizen must not worry themself with surveillance for they have nothing to hide. It is assumed that a desire for privacy stems from malintent. Yet this notion disregards privacy as a fundamental right of every citizen.

We need to develop a fresh perspective towards our privacy in the digital world. Consider the strict laws regarding search of private property. The state needs warrants to enter and search any property because the owner has the right to privacy in their personal space. Due to the intangible nature of cyberspace and the lack of unambiguous laws pertaining to it, this is often overlooked. The question then arises, should the state not require a warrant to surveil its citizen's private data? Abiding by the law must not be correlated with consenting to constant or incontestable monitoring of anyone’s private life. Our lives are irreversibly entangled with the digital world. There is an urgent need to formulate unequivocal regulations and laws that enlist the rights and govern the activities of the citizens as well as the state in the virtual world.
